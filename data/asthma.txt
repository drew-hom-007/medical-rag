Asthma is a chronic inflammatory condition of the airways characterised by reversible airflow obstruction and bronchospasm.

Common triggers of asthma include allergens such as pollen and dust mites, exercise, cold air, respiratory infections, and smoking.

Symptoms of asthma include wheezing, shortness of breath, chest tightness, and cough, particularly at night or early morning.

Diagnosis of asthma is confirmed by spirometry showing reversible airflow obstruction or a positive bronchodilator response.

Short-acting beta agonists such as salbutamol are used as reliever inhalers for immediate symptom relief during an asthma attack.

Inhaled corticosteroids such as beclomethasone are the most effective preventer treatment for persistent asthma and reduce airway inflammation.

The stepwise approach to asthma management adds treatments progressively based on symptom control and frequency of reliever inhaler use.

A peak flow meter measures how fast air can be exhaled and is used to monitor asthma control and detect deterioration early.

Severe asthma attacks require emergency treatment with high-dose salbutamol nebulisers, systemic corticosteroids, and controlled oxygen therapy.

Asthma action plans are written plans given to patients describing how to adjust medications based on symptoms and peak flow readings.
