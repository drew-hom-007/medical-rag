Community-acquired pneumonia is an infection of the lungs acquired outside of a hospital setting.

The most common cause of community-acquired pneumonia is Streptococcus pneumoniae, a type of bacteria.

Symptoms of pneumonia include productive cough, fever, chills, shortness of breath, and sharp chest pain when breathing.

Diagnosis is confirmed by chest X-ray showing consolidation, along with blood tests and sputum culture.

The CURB-65 score is used to assess pneumonia severity and guide treatment decisions based on confusion, urea, respiratory rate, blood pressure, and age.

Patients with a low CURB-65 score of 0 to 1 can be treated at home with oral antibiotics.

Patients with a CURB-65 score of 2 or more should be admitted to hospital for intravenous antibiotics.

First-line antibiotic treatment for community-acquired pneumonia is amoxicillin, with clarithromycin added for atypical organisms.

Oxygen therapy is given to pneumonia patients with oxygen saturations below 94 percent.

Prevention of pneumonia includes the pneumococcal vaccine, influenza vaccine, and smoking cessation.
